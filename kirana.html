The Research

5 interviews. One insight that changed everything.

Before opening Figma, I spent time with 5 kirana store owners in Bengaluru ‚Äî grocery shops, medical stores, a small electronics stall. I asked about their current tracking habits, what stressed them most, and what they'd actually use.

The pattern was immediate. Every owner tracked cash mentally, UPI from phone notifications, and credit in a physical notebook. By evening, reconciling these three systems was exhausting ‚Äî and most just gave up.

Core Insight

The real problem wasn't bad accounting

Existing apps tried to solve the accounting problem with more features: categories, reports, export to Excel. But that wasn't what owners needed. They needed to feel confident at the end of the day.

One believable daily number is more valuable than a detailed dashboard.
‚Äî Core insight from user research

Trust is built when the system matches their mental totals ‚Äî not when it offers more features. The anxiety wasn't caused by lack of data. It was caused by uncertainty about one number.

Psychology Framework

Three frameworks, three design decisions

My psychology background directly shaped the product. I didn't just cite frameworks ‚Äî I used them to make specific, testable decisions.

Cognitive Load Theory
Single Daily Number on home screen
Working memory holds 7¬±2 items. Kirana owners have 5‚Äì10 seconds between customers. A single "Today's Total" processes in 2‚Äì3 seconds; a multi-metric dashboard needs 30+ seconds they don't have. Decision: one number, nothing else on the home screen.

Loss Aversion (Kahneman & Tversky)
Neutral language in "Why this number?"
People feel losses twice as intensely as equivalent gains. When the app number doesn't match their mental total, blame triggers immediate abandonment (confirmed in research: "if wrong one day I delete it"). Decision: "Why this number?" uses neutral explanatory language ‚Äî never error messages or blame framing.
‚ö°
BJ Fogg's Behavior Model
Add Sale in under 3 seconds
Behavior = Motivation √ó Ability √ó Trigger. Owners are motivated (they want accurate totals) but exhausted (low ability). The trigger is a customer leaving. Decision: Add Sale requires only one number ‚Äî pre-filled ‚Çπ symbol, single input, one tap to confirm. Under 3 seconds. Near-zero friction means the habit actually forms.

What I Learned

Four things this project taught me

01
Simplicity requires discipline
I kept wanting to add features ‚Äî transaction history, categories, weekly reports. Every time I added something, I had to ask: "does this reduce the daily anxiety?" If not, it didn't belong. Removing features is harder than adding them.
02
Emotional outcomes matter more than functional ones
The goal wasn't accurate accounting. It was "do I feel confident at the end of the day?" My psychology background helped me frame the design problem correctly from the start ‚Äî around emotion, not features.
03
Testing reveals blind spots you can't predict
My original Add Sale input design looked broken to participants ‚Äî they thought it was a display glitch, not an input field. I couldn't have guessed that without testing. Test early, test rough, test often.
04
Constraints force better design
Limiting scope to one daily loop ‚Äî add sales, end day, see total ‚Äî forced focus on the core anxiety. More time might have led to over-designing. The best outcome of this project was knowing where to stop.
